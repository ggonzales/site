Development Planning
At the Agile Assurance Group, development planning following the Agile development methodology defines who we are, tapping our experienced engineers upfront to reduce time an cost to delivering your end product to market.
Development planning at Agile is detailed and fluid, comprehensive yet flexible enough to accommodate change throughout the development life cycle.


Planning puts on the table every known element and function envisioned to fulfill product requirements. In planning the known elements while able to pivot to address new complexities as they arise allows the product owner to prioritize, enabling the team to move forward while solving pitfalls, recognizing flaws and tackling them head on throughout the development life cycle through an evaluative, incremental, iterative process. It’s a holistic means of development by tackling pieces of the whole; counter intuitive to some, but inherent all of Agile’s services.

Planning iterations.
The foundation of development planning is to determine the length of iterations; shorter iterations lend urgency, whereas longer iterations can lull the team into a sense of unlimited time (and money). Iterations are normally two- to four-week periods. Agile believes it is critical to keep one goal in mind: consistent communication among all stakeholders ranking individuals above processes and tools results in the highest-functioning product in the least amount of time in the most efficient manner. Even if a release is not scheduled, iterations are the lifeblood of development and the lynchpin for planning to schedule research (architecture spikes), prototyping or user story creation for subsequent releases. When planning iterations, it’s critical to schedule retrospective sessions which include all stakeholders. Demonstrating features ensures lessons are learned for the next iteration; feedback leads to continual adjustment leading up to product release.

User story creation.
User stories are concise statements of customer requirements and are written by the entire team representing the work to be done to accomplish to accomplish product requirements. Project owners know what their customers want; a user story pinpoints with clarity basic customer needs with each product function broken down to the lowest common denominator. Epics contain numerous user stories pinpointing features at a higher level. Creation of user stories can be thought of as building blocks to reach product requirements, focusing on the most basic function to perform, and then creating a second user story with more complex function.

Product owner.
The product owner has a full understanding of customer needs and serves to ensure team members understand customer requirements. Agile planning allows team members to tackle features as ranked by the product owners. A close working relationship between product owner and team members is the art of Agile practices, allowing the team to focus on architecture work required to realistically predict time to release of functional components.

Product backlog.
In development planning, the product backlog is a prioritized list of user stories. Prioritizing the backlog allows the product owner to choose pieces of customer requirements critical to implementation; a single requirement could break down into a dozen user stories, but completion of a half dozen (in order of importance to the user) stories may meet user requirements, allowing the team to move to the next iteration. Identifying higher-level user stories and reducing them to story points allows the product owner to estimate time and cost, eliminating work that may seem necessary in planning, but becomes immaterial based on desired function.

Release backlog.
The product owner, using the product backlog, identifies user stories to be included in the next release, placing them in the release backlog. Tracking team velocity (team accomplishments measured by time) ensures proper estimates on what can and can’t be accomplished. A customer team (product manager, testers, real users, interaction designers) can break down high-level user stories into smaller chunks, prioritizing them in the release backlog.

Iteration backlog.
The team must select high-priority items from the release backlog and place them in the next iteration. During development planning of the iteration backlog, team members examine specific tasks for each user story. When conducing planning development meetings, breaking down each task required for each user story is critical, estimating hours to accomplish each task and avoiding over commitment of resources. Tasks for each team member must be identified. When Agile uses the Agile method of development planning, it recognizes that flexibility – allowing for change throughout the process – is a key advantage. Recognizing that that a user story is more complicated than originally planned and can’t realistically be contained in the iteration, team members raise the issue immediately. Additional user stories may be needed in hopes of to find an interim solution and meet the release deadline. The shortcoming is then noted and can be addressed in detail. If an architecture spike is needed for any user story during the iteration, user stories are added to the release backlog to be prioritized by the product owner.

Measuring velocity.
Agile development planning hinges on tracking velocity – how much work is done by a team during iterations. Basically, points are awarded for each user story completed, and no points for failing to deliver on a story. By charting the points earned in iterations, the numbers can be used to determine the time to attack the remaining release backlog by each team member in the next iteration.
