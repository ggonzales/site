Business Analysis
All software projects start in similar ways.
They begin as concepts that are either rooted in addressing a business or social need, innovating or automating an existing process, reengineering previous software versions, or creating an entirely new paradigm. No matter which of these categories your software requirements fall under – you must first comprehensively understand the needs of the finished product.

Often our clients come to us with thorough documentation that blueprints their entire project. In these instances, it’s recommendable that the documentation undergoes a Quality Assurance Audit to assure the project plan and feature set addresses all known User Requirements of the final product. Other times we are tasked with doing the Business Analysis on behalf of the client. Whether or not The Agile Assurance Group authors your Requirements – it is always prudent to include QA.

From a high-level view, Business Analysis includes:

  • Defining the goals of the software project
  • Defining the needs of the entity creating the software – Business Requirement Documentation
  • Defining the needs of the users – User Requirement Documentation
  • Defining the feature set to address the needs of the Business and User Requirements – Functional Requirement Documentation
  • Definition of scalability, performance, usability, and security – Quality of Service Documentation
  • Infusion of Quality Assurance to assess risk factors in the requirements and to assure the goals and needs of business, users, and features are aligned
For those who are not practiced in these disciplines, the tasks seem mundane yet intimidating. In short, it’s the part of the process most Project Owners are not excited to complete or even start. The level of effort required to execute the Business Analysis will depend on the size of the project and its importance to your organization. For mission-critical programs, nothing short of an exhaustive effort is in order.

The importance of proper Business Analysis and its associated QA practices cannot be understated.

 Imagine a multi-million dollar project which is executed flawlessly, on time, and on budget – yet does not address the company’s needs. This may sound like an extreme example, but it does happen and it’s not rare. The Agile Assurance Group excels in Business Analysis and can offer you the extra layer of protection your product demands.

Although we apply best practices in Agile Software Development and can accommodate changes in requirements even late in the development process – fewer changes are still desirable. Most changes in requirements effect delivery schedules and budgets. The truth is there are very few, if any, software projects that are delivered as originally specified due to changes requested along the way. No one has perfect foresight, even the very best business analysts in the industry. Regardless of that fact, you will benefit greatly from having changes that are smaller in scope and quantity as opposed to having many big changes.

The Business Analyst has ongoing roles after their documentation has been approved. Some of the more common are:

  • To serve as an intermediary between Project Owners and Project Managers
  • Participation in User Acceptance Testing
  • Participation in Development Planning to ensure the sequence of deliverables meets business needs, among other things
  • Support of implementation and training
  • Support of post-implementation protocol
