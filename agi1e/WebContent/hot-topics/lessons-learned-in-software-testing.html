Lessons Learned in Software Testing
Relying on an Agile Assurance Group’s software testing services saves our clients time, money and allows companies to excel in their core entrepreneurial endeavors. Having quality testers involved throughout the Software Development Life Cycle is crucial to reducing defects, increasing time- to- market, reduces post-release technical support overhead and reduces overall product development costs.

Agile’s experience has resulted in numerous lessons learned in software testing which we pass to our clients in all we do. Those lessons include:

  • Stuff happens. During each stage of the software development life cycle, errors are inevitable. In many projects, testing accounts for 40 percent of development costs. When it comes to product quality and development cost efficiencies, top-tier software testing is essential. Test planning, analysis and design, implementation, iterative and end-product testing prior to final release hinges on a solid software testing plan.
  • Agile testing. Following Agile methods of testing allows for the incorporation of frequent change. Following the Agile methodology is incremental rather than sequential, relying on individual skill and team communication over tools and methods.
  • Automated testing saves money. If the test is repetitive, evaluates high-risk conditions, is impossible or costly to perform manually, requires multiple data values to perform the same action, and is a baseline on several different configurations, automation is the best practice.
  • Quality investment. Spending on software testing pays off throughout the development life cycle. When establishing a testing budget, it’s imperative to recognize the value of testing in return-on-investment. Testing isn’t just a necessary evil; it’s the heart of Agile’s services with a huge payoff for our clients.
  • External failures cost more. If a tester doesn’t find the bug, the customer will. Customer discovered errors make fixes exponentially more expensive, far more than internal failure, programmer-found or tester-found errors, making technical support overheard soar, with clients absorbing the cost of fixing a defect when the product is in the field. Agile is in the business of satisfying customers, not raising budgets. Applying testing in a rigorous Quality Assurance plan, allowing testing to evolve at each iteration completion, results in the best-quality product to hit the market.
  • Smart testing. With quality programming and development experience, Agile’s testers know which testing principles to apply and when, first by examining how various testing methods – regression and acceptance testing, exploratory and scenario testing, requirements-based testing as well as requirements stability and traceability – results t in the least defects for each project. The goal is best testing practices and efficient test design with efficient test case documentation.
  • Minimize risk. Agile’s testing methods, achieved over decades of trial and error, minimizes risk by incrementally developing software, iteration like a miniature project of its own, testing along the way for incremental functionality.
  • Project’s headlights. A software tester sees risk down the road, lights the way for iterations, illuminates the way for programmers and managers, highlighting anticipated bumps in the road. Software testing incorporates programming experience with testing expertise, predicting pitfalls prior to code implementation, incrementally testing as coding continues, and releasing a potential incrementally shippable product.
  • Find the important bugs; ignore the insignificant. No software is defect free. Core functions must be tested before contributing functions. Capability must be tested first before reliability. Common threats need to be found before exotic threats, high-impact problems before low-impact issues.
  • Testers serve many. Project managers must know the testing team’s processes, receiving timely reports on demand and raising awareness of bugs quickly. Testers serve the programmer with timely, solid defect reports without wasting the programmer’s time or creating project bottlenecks. Technical support would be impossible without solid defect reports post release; technical support teams must be alerted to any problems the user could experience under all known scenarios. Marketing must be aware of any potential problems inhibiting product requirements for users. Ultimately, the product owner is the ultimate user, demanding crisp test reports in operational terms allowing the owner to pivot in place, adapting to issues learned in software testing.


The biggest lessons learned in software testing are inherent in the Agile Software Development method: individuals and communication are more important than process and tools; working software is more valuable than over-bearing, paper-laden and inefficient documentation reliance; customer interaction and involvement is more important than contract negotiations,; the adaptability to change is the single biggest contributor to end product quality and has the biggest impact on our clients’ bottom line.
