Agile Process
Agile Software Development is an applied philosophy of iterative and incremental software development.
It adheres to a rigorous formula of holistic integration and on-the-spot product improvement as opposed to documenting development at the conclusion of coding and sifting through the data for errors, compatibility and functionality. The Agile software development process requires cross-functional teams taking ownership and responsibility for their planning, deliverables, and timelines under the supervision of professional project management. The Agile Software Development methodology is the foundation for nearly all activities undertaken by the Agile Assurance Group.

Agile isn’t just our name – it’s what we do.

Whether Agile is tasked with developing a client’s software or augmenting the client’s development team with our quality control and assurance practices, you will see the “Agile philosophy” is our guiding principle.

The underlying principles of Agile Software Development are intuitive to most IT professionals, yet rarely applied in a holistic fashion. The Manifest for Agile Software Development, published in 2001, is widely considered to be the bible for efficient software development.

The principles of Agile Software Development’s manifesto are best summarized by Agile Assurance Group’s checklist:

Rapid Delivery
projects broken into “milestones” and “sprints.” Sprints are incremental deadlines within a project’s overall goals. Sprints typically last between one and three weeks and produce a potentially shippable incremental product.

Constant interaction
between Agile and our clients is a key distinction between Agile and other development methodologies. Instead of relying solely on documentation to produce a product, the Agile philosophy requires that Project Owner’s participation in daily Scrum sessions.

Change Requirements
are accommodated even late in development. This is another key difference between Agile Software Development and other methods and another reason close client interaction is required. The ability to accommodate change is the essence of Agile.

Cooperation
among various locations of project team members requires daily, consistent, efficient communication throughout the development lifecycle.

Sustainable
software development is a must. Even though development increments are called sprints, project planning must allow for realistic goals within realistic deadlines.

Successful
teams are comprised of talented, trusted staff motivated to practice technical excellence and superior design.

Incrementally
working software is the primary measure of our success.
The steps in Agile Assurance Group’s typical sprint process include:

Planning
A technical team leader decides which features and requirements should be taken from a detailed schedule and incorporated in the sprint. The decision on which features included in each sprint will be dictated by architecture, dependencies, milestones and resource availability.

Defining Tracks
This is an integral part of the planning process. A track is a succession of interdependent feature requirements. Typically, developers focus on one track and do not move to another until reaching the sprint or milestone. The number of tracks that can be identified limits waste of development resources.

Story Writing
This is a critical part of the development and sprint process in a sophisticated, mature service delivery model. This is where individual developers take ownership for their work and their estimates. A story is a subset of requirements where a developer writes out the required functions from a user perspective, then estimates the amount of time to complete the specific job. Stories also establish dependencies within the feature and become an easy and reliable tool to estimate the effects of changes in user requirements.

Test Planning
Quality assurance professionals determine where the key risks are in the sprint and the best way to test and mitigate problems. QA Engineers typically assist Developers in story writing to ensure user functional requirements and design are accounted for.

Execution
This is the phase of the sprint where development, QA, testing, and client interaction comes together. By constant interaction among all stakeholders and daily meetings to assure requirements are being met, the team remains on target to ship an incremental, working product. Burn down charts is used to track progress versus goals on a daily basis.

Acceptance testing
This is where the rubber meets the road. Quality control measures performed jointly by the Project owner and our QA staff are performed on incrementally shippable product at the end of the sprint. The results of acceptance testing determine the success of the sprint. Acceptance testing is done with an eye toward the completeness of the incremental product compared to the requirements and designs.

Post-sprint meetings
These face-to-face sessions of all team members are conducted at the conclusion of every sprint. These are especially valuable in the case of failed sprints. Retrospective meetings are where the entire team learns from their successes and failures and adjust performance for upcoming sprints. Retrospective meetings determine what worked and what didn’t, which practices should be dropped and what lessons were learned.
